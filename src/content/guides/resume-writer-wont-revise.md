You did the responsible thing. You paid a professional — maybe a few hundred dollars — to make your resume land. The first draft came back looking suspiciously like your old resume with new fonts. You sent notes. One revision arrived, sort of addressing them. You sent more notes. Then the replies slowed down, or stopped, or you got told that's the final draft.

If that's where you are, this guide is for you. Three steps: salvage, push once more (the right way), and know when to rebuild.

## First, know what you were owed

A real rewrite — at any price — should have added things that weren't on your resume before:

- **Named specifics.** The actual tools, systems, standards, and training your work involved — the words a recruiter searches, not "various software."
- **The right target.** A resume aimed at the job you're going *toward*, in that field's language — not a shinier version of the job you're leaving.
- **Real numbers.** Honest scale and results pulled out of you by good questions, not invented for you.
- **Clean machine-readability.** A file that hiring software can actually parse.

If what you got back is your own sentences rearranged, the service didn't rewrite — it reformatted. That gap is what you're fixing now.

## Step 1 — Salvage what's worth keeping

Don't torch the paid draft. Even a disappointing rewrite usually has something worth saving:

- A cleaner structure or section order than your original
- A few genuinely better-phrased lines
- A tidy education/certification block

Copy those into a "keep" list. The rescue checklist below walks the draft section by section so you can separate keep, fix, and missing in about twenty minutes.

## Step 2 — Ask for changes that can't be dodged

If you want one more attempt from the writer, make the request impossible to half-do. Vague notes ("make it stronger") get vague revisions. Instead:

- **One change per note, tied to a line.** "Bullet 3 under Acme: I led the migration, I didn't 'assist' — please rewrite it that way."
- **Give the missing fact.** "Add that I held an OSHA 30 card — it's not in the draft anywhere."
- **Name the target.** "This reads like a warehouse resume. I'm applying to dispatch roles — the language needs to point there."

Precise notes are fair to the writer and give you a clean record of what you asked for. If precise notes still come back ignored, you have your answer.

## Step 3 — Know when to stop chasing

Rebuild instead of arguing when you see these signs:

- Two rounds of specific notes, still your old resume in new clothes
- The draft could belong to anyone in your field — nothing in it is specifically *you*
- It's aimed at the wrong role and the writer keeps polishing instead of re-aiming
- The replies have stopped and your money is spent either way

At that point, more emails cost you weeks you could spend applying.
